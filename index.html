OCT - 11

My arm was twitching terribly again, so I decided to give up napping any longer. Above my roof, the sky was covered by its own roof of clouds, darkening the drab neighborhood. The house I live in must look like a piece of Ikea furniture in a thrift shop - the square building was recently repainted and lined with crisply trimmed little bushes, while the rest of the street only has scraggly trees and facades that are peeling. I’d noticed it when the gardener had forgotten to trim a stray branch, and I completed the job with a pair of scissors. For some reason, I began to feel like it was my secret, and now I check behind the bush every day when I’m back from work at EARTHDATA. Sometimes it looks slightly different than the previous day, like a signature re-written with the same hand.

I held my phone to my face and my eyes watered from the screen’s brightness. The days have been shorter lately, but clouds hid any indication of time passing. It was already within the NIGHT timeframe, I’d replied to Data Requests in the MORNING and slept on the sofa through the AFTERNOON. I think Daniel and I texted during the MIDNIGHT time. Well, I had breakfast with Dallas Raines and tested my homemade geodesist station at Signal Hill

I remember enjoying the rest of our conversation, as it was filled with familiar adventure, without his usual dramatics. could I stop by tonight?

For my extremely late lunch, I heated up a bowl of soup I’d made the day before and poured some crackers in. I didn’t ask Daniel why he wanted to visit. Perhaps he wanted to explain his extraordinarily eventful week in person. I returned to my laptop to check my emails, occasionally freeing my eyes to the window. There was a tiny patch of sky I hadn’t noticed before, revealing the true, deep navy color of the sky. While sending several data requests, I kept glancing at it until it was overtaken by its neighbors.

“Hey E! Wow, you look like you’ve been asleep all day.” He gave me a light hug.

“Yeah, most of it.”

“So what’s up with this week?” I asked.

He swallowed the last segment and said,

“Well, I actually wanted to ask you a question.”

“Yeah?” This was very unusual, and I found myself generating possible directions, all of which lead to an undefined answer.

“Well, have you ever counted how many words there are total in all the texts we’ve sent each other?”

I doubted this was actually the question. This wasn’t important and his would take up a much higher word count. I shook my head.

He motioned that he was going to sit down.

Daniel has slightly above average length legs that remind me of an insect when he sits cross-legged. Today though, we sat on the couch. We finished the oranges, then he turned towards me and gave a humble expression, and I could’ve sworn I noticed slight worry holding up his eyebrows.

“Thanks for always feeding me by the way, E. You’re the only person who always gives me healthy food, and I need it.”

I said it was no problem, it was nice to share my snacks with people. This still wasn’t a question though.

“But, you’re, um, always casual and generous with everything. I really enjoy relaxing with you.”

“Oh, thanks.” Nice, but typical compliment assortment; he’d told me many times that his favorite time of the day were evenings with me. But his expression made me uneasy. His brows kept skewing, quivering slightly.

“I’ve been unable to work properly lately, I’ve been terribly distracted.”

“Are you worried about something?”

I double checked that my own forehead was relaxed, free of the slight crease that sometimes appeared above my brow.

His brows raised further upwards, then he straightened his face out and looked serious.

He finally asked, “Well, E, would you want to go out with me?”

My stomach felt like it was condensing into a small sphere. I can’t. Not only is my newest job occupying most of the diminishing daylight, but he’s a friend to me to the same degree as Penny and sometimes I doubt we’ve ever had a conversation. I am not sure if he knows what my weeks are actually like, while I could list a detailed record of each of his.

I told him I'd think about it.

Penny texted me later, and as soon as I told her he’d visited, she surprised me: Oh, did he come over to at last reveal his friendship had turned romantic??
She knew what he had been feeling. I wonder if she happens to know how I feel.

Getting sleepy now, signing off.

OCT - 12

“You know, Woman and Coffee or something like that.”

“So how are you handling this? Have you responded to him yet?”

“I always thought you two had a bond, or at least inside jokes I never understood. And you know his parents.”

“What’s that odd face about?”

“Oh, my coffee is already lukewarm.” I took a sip and blinked.

“Sorry I kept you waiting. Have an influx of data projects this week.”

“I do hope you’re ok, Esse. It must be strange, as you practically lost a best friend. Remember you can call me whenever you need reassurance or guidance on anything, or just a person around.”

“Thanks Penny, I really am fine.” I swallowed my last drop of coffee. Despite the tenseness of my stomach it felt hollow.

“Alright.” That was the end of her motherly advice for the day. Thankfully, she set the topic aside.

“Did you ever end up listening to the Impromptu piano works of Schubert?”

I nodded and formed my hands to a perfect circle around the base. Her and Daniel have distinct music tastes. She surrounds me by her preference of classical classics - Tchaikovsky, Shastokovich, Schubert, Brahms, and Debussy. Daniel pulls me in the other direction, introducing me to obscure punk rock bands and inviting me to small shows throughout downtown. Lately though, I’ve been replaying music that you’d probably find on an elevator, or in a hotel lobby. Plain stuff that never really asks to be listened to, and doesn’t have much of a melody; yet I could listen to it all day.

“He was quite extraordinary - did I ever tell you how he composed his works? He stopped using a piano, just wrote the notes straight on the sheets. So crazy and talented, he thought the piano got in the way. I couldn’t imagine working without my piano equivalent, skipping an outline before writing anything.”

Penny has been generating articles for EARTHNOW JOURNAL for probably 4 years, covering philosophy and everything related in her direct and intense style. I can’t imagine her writing without a detailed series of paragraphs. Compared to my writing, which is mostly unedited - due to it being a hobby I can only attend to on weekends.

“Not everyone works like Schubert,” I remarked with a thoughtful expression, trying to remember whether my latest favorite track had any piano in it.

“Very true.”

Penny emptied her own mug and had to report to a project coordinator. She still had to polish an upcoming article, so she left to work at her home office, while I stuck around the shop to finish writing this alone. It’s now the NIGHT timeframe. I think I’m going to sign off, so the barista doesn’t start thinking I’m homeless. Never enough time to write it seems; I wish my shifts weren’t so long during the week.

I pushed aside the refrigerator to examine myself in the mirror. Last night’s earthquake had rearranged the house into an Escher spectacle and relocated it several islands away, but everything inside was still here. My right shoulder was still out of place, making my silhouette appear constantly leaning to the side.

The mirror shrunk with a clink and attached to my wristband, the surface becoming pixelated like a digital wrist watch. It beeped and digital letters scrolled across the screen: GOOD MIDNIGHT EZR- when the second earthquake began. Grabbing onto the kitchen sink, I twisted my wrist and heard the screen crack slightly, and instinctively let go. The world shook me out the window, sending me past the mansions, airports, grocery stores, and neighborhoods, all unempathetic and distant as crisply-painted dollhouses.

I finally landed in a gridded space that resembled a desperately clean white-tiled bathroom. Ahead of me, a path of green-and-blue tiles began, and led me to a dimly lit, curving escalator. As I descended, the tiles spread above me to become a bright blue day lined with greenery, that seemed to embrace me with familiarity. When I reached the bottom, the escalator froze, and I realized I was at my 27th office.

A few steps in front of me a narrow creek, or used-to-be-creek, since it was now a dried pathway of dusty rocks, encircled a willow tree. Flowing from the tree, a bedroom-size veil of branches hung heavily, starved of a breeze. I pushed aside the veil and entered the office. I blinked and hardly recognized it, as it had become a sort of storage space for all kinds of office things, being crowded with dressers, files, paper towers, books, boxes, a computer, printer, and a desk with an irrational amount of pens.

Pushing aside some boxes, I pulled out my notebook and stubby pencil and began cataloguing what I found. I couldn’t help but notice the stacks of literature were very outdated and hardly relevant anymore. Torn, yellowed editions of Jane Eyre, Pride and Prejudice, and Much Ado About Nothing. I was skimming the last pages of an edition of The Tale of Two Cities, when something shuffled around. Thinking it was in the desk, I pulled open the drawer and pushed things around but couldn’t find it. As I bent underneath and touched the back panel, it shook again with more clarity; it was inside a hidden compartment behind the panel. With closer inspection, I noticed a keyhole, centered in a brass oval about thumb-size, and a sapphire glass knob attached below. Keys were nowhere in clear sight, so I impatiently yanked at the knob, to no avail. Pressing my face against the worn wooden surface, I tried to simply listen. The contents gently shook the whole desk and my head which was now connected to it, and something like the trapped wind in a seashell moved inside.

Earth tremors today.

I didn’t do any work but Daniel had one last Collection Request, so I just tried writing a poem while he was out. Soon we’ll both be absorbed in our busy lives again, since officials are saying that trains will be working by tomorrow. When he came back, we gathered up things to go on a picnic at Earthwell Park. It’s a wide, expansive park, with a lake and a stream that cuts through, clusters of romantic willow trees, and a community garden providing a colorful display of flowers. Daniel and I sat on a blanket under a willow and ate the sandwiches and fruits I’d prepared. We were picking from a bowl of strawberries when the branches began to shake. Daniel was wrapped up around me again, despite the lack of ceilings or walls that could collapse on us. It felt unlike the previous ones; something was testing the ground, each tremor slightly different in movement. I counted the length of each; the first was 2 seconds, then 3, 5, 7, 5, and 3 - like a weird pattern. I thought there’d be another 2 second one, but it remained still. I turned to Daniel. He faced me and drew closer.

I noticed my arm was twitching again, and I was slightly shaking. Ever since the first earthquake my center-sphere has not regained it original composure. The anxiety is freely running through my veins. I know Daniel thinks I’m terrified, despite that I’ve calmly denied it. I shake even when the ground is stable; earlier, we were walking through the flowers and I couldn’t make it stop. He held my hand when he noticed it, and explained the directional relationships between the tectonic plates and EARTHDATA’s unfailing ability to keep them at peace in an effort to ease me.

As he drew close to me on the blanket, I realized he wanted to kiss me. I was unsure. I knew it was a date, but I felt no romance. I pulled back slightly, which he noticed.

“Thinking about EARTHDATA again? Forget about them. We have each other, right?” He said, combing my hair.

I smiled and let him kiss me.

OCT - 23 - part 1

I don’t understand. I’m surrounded by collapsed walls and floors, the station is in rubble, and electricity is unreliable, due to generators being damaged. And still I can see out of this little break room window that people are casually walking down the street, focused on their Datanows. The news calmly explained it as a 6.0, recoverable disaster. I called Penny and Daniel to check in with them, and they clearly believed it. I don’t anymore. Whatever EARTHNEWS reports to us, it is not aligning with what I’m experiencing.

This morning I prepared for my second day back at the office since last week. Daniel wanted to stay a bit longer with me, as he has been concerned about my nervous tremors. I think he’s afraid I’ll collapse too. I also think I am beginning to get used to our relationship. He kissed me at the station, before I boarded my train. I just realized, I hadn’t seen the plain man on the bench today. Yet I did yesterday, which I find odd.

At work, I received a tsunami of Data Sorting to do, so I immediately entered the Datanow Reality-to-System Room, and remained through my lunch break and a few hours overtime. I almost finished the overdue assignments, but before I could, all the data suddenly faded and disappeared. I stepped out and everything began to shake violently. I stumbled to the floor, and crawled under a desk. There was no shouting, just cracking and crashing sounds as lights flashed and dust piled up.

Now I’m in the break room, wondering what to do next. Most of the ground is a maze of insulation rubble. It seems nearly everyone had already gone home, since I didn’t hear any voices. Or whoever was here had been crushed now. It was lucky I was doing Sorting today, since the desks are newer and sturdier in the DNRS room than around the Systems office.

I’m shaking, but the twitching is gone. Now to decide what to do. I think I’ll try to find my way down and out of the building. I’ve never walked around this area but perhaps there’s a B&B still intact enough.

OCT - 23 - part 2

I knew this place was a feat of architecture, about the size of a small town in which you never need to go outside, since even the station is connected through a concrete tunnel (which is probably destroyed now). Yet I cannot exit the building, or find the exit. I did find that Datanews HQ is the next division over within this structure, so I found myself wandering down the hallways which were unblocked by debris. I haven’t been interrupted by any other curious victims; it seems everyone cleared out at their shift’s end. As I made my way down the half-lit corridors, it hit me that this was my chance to resolve my suspicions.

The interiors remind me of an outdated airport, although without any stores. Instead, there is an occasional door, windowless and labeled with dark green numbers. All were locked, and made a dense metal sound when I tapped on them. At last I came to a door which was unhinged and leaning off the frame. It was not numbered, but instead had ‘Datanews SR Room’ written on the label. I do RS, so SR would mean System-to-Reality. Which is odd, and could be the evidence I’m searching for. Now I am staring the equipment inside. It looks identical to my own - the Immersion Box is above the panel, which is imbedded in the streamline-shaped desk. The only differences are the label, reading DNSR, and the chair, being deep green and in better condition than my own. It is still on - a dot of light on the box flashes in saturated red. Daniel or Penny might try to believe me, for the sake of empathy, but they love their jobs. They’d never be able to risk it. I’m going to enter the DNSR - wish me luck.
